It all begins with a recent German case. In that case, a German girl, then 15-years-old, died of a rail road accident in 2012. Proceedings were brought and one key matter of fact is whether the girl was committing suicide at the time.

II A UNIVERSAL RIGHT

The argument by Facebook and its supporters might be a bit of â€˜childishâ€™. But clearly, it represents young peopleâ€™s idea. It is common practice for teenagers to claim â€˜privacyâ€™ when parents tried to enter into their rooms. For them, privacy is a weapon against the paternal authorities.

However, privacy is definitely not a privilege of teenagers. Just like teenagers, privacy is also a weapon for the general public, against government authorities. In a legal sense, the right, or more precisely the protection, of privacy, is universal. This universality is manifested in the modern recognition of privacy as a basic human right.

Perhaps some of the most important moments in the modern recognition of privacy as a basic human right are the drafting and signing of international human rights covenants, where privacy was written into. Specifically, there are 3 materials that emphasised explicitly the right to privacy, namely: (1) Article 12 in the Universal Declaration on Human Rights (UDHR); and (2) Article 17 of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR); and (3) Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR).

These Articles underpins the importance of privacy. However, this importance emphasised here contrasts with the uncertainties about its conceptual basis. One can find nothing from those Articles that suggests the nature of the right. For example, Art 12 of the UDHR stated that:
No one should be subjected to arbitrary interference with his privacy, family, home or correspondence, nor to attacks on his honour or reputation. Everyone has the right to the protection of the law against such interferences or attacks.
Those vague words teaches us nothing about the nature of the right to privacy. Art 17 ICCPR used similar words. ECHR focused on â€˜freedom from governmental interferenceâ€™, yet still provide nothing for the understanding of privacy. This is not incidental. Studies on the drafting history of these sources have shown that drafters did not intend to give a definition of privacy. Rather, the terms â€˜right to privacyâ€™ were inserted into those Articles coincidentally rather than intentionally. In those contexts, the â€˜right to privacyâ€™ is a catch-all word for many undefined rights (in Diggelmann and Cleisâ€™s words, an â€˜umbrellaâ€™).

Overall, the right to privacy is universal. Yet the nature, content, and scope of the right to privacy is still uncertain. One still need to go to domestic law to find the nature of privacy as a legal concept.

III PRESERVATION OF THE PRIVATE SPHERE

As the word itself suggests, privacy is a preservation of the private sphere. A brief n of the history of privacy suggests that the protection of oneâ€™s private life from intrusion, either from public authorities, or from being published.

In one famous article â€˜The Two Western Cultures of Privacy: Dignity Versus Libertyâ€™, James Whitman in this article identified 2 norms that characterised privacy law: dignity and liberty. Continental countries, like French and Germany, place privacy law on the basis of ideas of â€˜personal honorâ€™, or the ambition to guarantee everyoneâ€™s position in society. American law, on the other hand, shows a great sensitivity to â€˜personhoodâ€™. As in the famous article â€˜The Right to Privacyâ€™, Samuel Warren and Louis Brandeis characterised the right to privacy as the â€˜right to be let aloneâ€™.

The 2 norms have later been formulated as dignity v liberty. For the 2 values, different threatens are of primary concern: A European interest in personal dignity, threatened primarily by the mass media; An American interest in liberty, threatened primarily by the government.

IV A COMMON LAW REVIEW

Similar distinctions have been seen in common law jurisdictions. The approach to the protection given to privacy is different between the US and other common law jurisdictions that followed the precedent of the UK.

A US Position: Tort Law Right of Privacy, Especially Against the Government

In the US, privacy has developed as a common law right based on tort law.

The development of privacy as a legal concept in the US started in 1890, when the landmark article, â€˜The Right to Privacyâ€™ by Warren and Brandeis, was published. Their primary concern was the emerging trend since the late 1800s of the public disclosure of private life by the newspapers or other media. During that time, newspapers started heavily peeping on oneâ€™s private lives (usually lives of those celebrities) and report their gossips or scandals, in order to attract readers and to increase sale. Warren and Brandeis commented with reference to this phenomenon that â€˜[g]ossip is no longer the resource of the idle and of the vicious, but has become a trade, which is pursued with industry as well as effronteryâ€™. They then went through many judicial or non-judicial materials, and found that none of the existing areas of law offered appropriate protection for privacy. They analysed the law of defamation, law of property and law of intellectual property, and concluded that none of them were suitable for the protection of privacy. Therefore, they came up with the conclusion that the right to privacy should be a new category of common law rights, i.e. â€˜the more general right of the individual to be let aloneâ€™. The proper remedy for the breach of that general right, should be an â€˜action of tort for damagesâ€™.

This was followed in the 1960s by William Prosser, a well-known tort law scholar. In his famous article â€˜Privacyâ€™, he analysed more than 300 cases â€˜that were spawned by the Warren and Brandeis articleâ€™ after 1890, and categorised them into 4 kinds of torts:
(1) intrusion upon seclusion;
(2) public disclosure of private facts;
(3) false light or â€˜publicityâ€™;
(4) appropriation.
Among the 4 categories, 3 led directly to the concern of â€˜publicity of privacyâ€™, with the remaining one focused on identity theft. In this sense, privacy, according to Lawrence Friedman, as a tort law â€˜protection from the despicable noisiness of the mediaâ€™, has been well-established in common law.

Moreover, right to privacy has also long been recognised in the US as a constitutional right against the government, vested in the Fourth Amendment.

The Fourth Amendment said:
The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated, and no Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause, supported by Oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the place to be searched, and the persons or things to be seized.

Despite the general perception of the Fourth Amendment that right to privacy should be a shield for individuals against government surveillances, none of the words in that Amendment, however, indicated a right to privacy. For centuries the application of Fourth Amendment had been rare. And courts tended to read it narrowly, i.e. in terms of trespass only. However, the Warren and Brandeis article â€˜The Right to Privacyâ€™, though generally a response to the increasing invasion of private life by newspapers, had a seed against government intrusions upon private life. And it finally grew up.

Brandeis later became a Justice of the Supreme Court of the US, and was involved in a Fourth Amendment claim case called Olmstead v United States. In that case, the claimant was convict of conspiracy. However, the prosecution was relying on evidences obtained from wiretapping. The majority of the court dismissed the appeal. Taft CJ delivered the majority judgment, saying that the wiretappings had not violated the Fourth Amendment, in the sense that those talking on the phone were voluntarily engaged in. In other words, violation of Fourth Amendment is confined to trespassing into the property only.

3 judges dissent in this case and wrote separate opinions. Brandeis was one of them. â€˜The right to be let aloneâ€™, he used the term once again, and said it has been violated by the illegal wiretaps by the government. Brandeisâ€™s dissent opinion was just an early attempt to read the right to privacy into Fourth Amendment. These dissent opinions survived, when the majority decision in Olmstead v United States has been overturned later in 1967, and even prospered thereafter. In Katz, the Supreme Court expanded the scope of Fourth Amendment, and said: â€˜what [a person] seeks to preserve as private, even in an area accessible to the public, may be constitutionally protectedâ€™.

B UK Position: A Relationship of Trust And Confidence

On the contrary, in jurisdictions like UK or Australia, there is no â€˜right to privacyâ€™ based on tort law. Instead, protection of privacy is based on equity. In these jurisdictions, wrongful publication is characterised as a breach of confidential information, and thus are protected by law of confidence.

Yet, as many scholars have noticed, privacy is different from confidentiality, and it may be not so appropriate to utilise the equitable principle of confidence to protect privacy.

In order for equitable doctrines of confidence to apply, the information should generally be â€˜communicated in a confidential wayâ€™. In scenarios of breach of confidence, there is usually a recipient of that confidential information. The duty and liability of confidence comes from â€˜circumstances as to import an obligation of confidenceâ€™ upon the recipient of that information. For example, in Mense v Milenkovic, McInerney J said that â€˜if â€¦ [under an objective test] â€¦ the recipient of the information would have realised that upon reasonable grounds the information was being given to him in confidence, then this should suffice to impose upon him the equitable obligation of confidenceâ€™.

Privacy, on the other hand, does not necessarily need any recipients. Indeed, it is common that people sometimes share their â€˜secretsâ€™ (often â€˜privacyâ€™) to friends, yet imposed an obligation to confidence on the listener, with the prior statement â€˜I want to tell you one secret of mine, but donâ€™t tell othersâ€™. But that is only one of many scenarios where potential breach of privacy might arise. Sometimes they may not share their privacy at all, and this lack of communication definitely does not affect the protection of their privacy as they deserved. Protection of privacy should be based on the nature of the information itself, not in the act of confidential communication.

In these jurisdictions, the limitations and improperness of placing privacy under law of confidence has been detected by scholars. Consequently, some of them argued in favour of the recognition of a tort law chose of action for invasion into othersâ€™ privacy. However, the court in those jurisdictions have never adopted that position. Australian courts, for example, have â€˜not welcomed any judicial initiative to recognise a tort of privacyâ€™, though they have modified the test slightly and tend to ignore the requirement of communication when faced with privacy issues. Overall, currently, protection of privacy is still under the law of confidence in these jurisdictions.

The difference in the understanding of the nature of privacy has also influenced the practice in the 2 jurisdictions. In the US, claims of right to privacy has been tightly connected with evidence law cases. Leading Fourth Amendment cases in America, such as Katz, United States v Jones, and Kyllo v United States, are all criminal cases where the defendant would be found guilty but for the illegality in the evidence used by the prosecution. In one recent book, Habeas Data: Privacy vs. the Rise of Surveillance Tech, Cyrus Farivar analysed 10 US cases and came to similar sentiments.

In the UK, on the other hand, leading case law tended to focus on wrongful publications. For example, in Theakston v MGN Ltd, the claimant was a TV host for programs directed at teenagers and youngsters. The defendant published photos and reports that the claimant had visited a brothel. The court held that this did not amount to privacy and decline to grant injunction preventing the publication. On the contrary, in Campbell v MGN Ltd, the court held that the publication of the attendance of a Narcotics Anonymous meeting by a â€˜supermodelâ€™ was an invasion of privacy. Those key cases have all focused on preventing publication.

Despite those distinctions, the 2 jurisdictions have come to similar end, though coming through different paths. After developments and evolutions in law, both jurisdictions have come up with the same test in deciding whether it is privacy and whether protection should be imposed.

In the US, the test was first introduced in Katz. The fact in this case was similar to that of Olmstead. The claimant, Katz, was a handicapper. He used a bank of 3 telephone booths at the corner of a street and make callings to book his choice every day. The FBI put microphones in the phone booths and eavesdropped on his conversations. This recording was used as evidence of Katzâ€™s gambling. The Supreme Court held that although the phone booths is in a public place, the use of it is confined in a zone that may lead a person to expect it to be private. On this ground, Katzâ€™s phone call was privacy, since he has a â€˜reasonable expectation of privacyâ€™. The test, as articulated by Harlan J, is a 2-step test:
a. Whether the person has an â€˜actual or subjective expectation of privacyâ€™; if so, then
b. Whether â€˜the expectation â€¦ [is] one that society is prepared to recognize as â€œreasonableâ€â€™.
In this case, Katzâ€™s expectation is held to be a reasonable one. If he speaks so loud that that the whole street can hear his voice, then it cannot be a privacy because even if he has that expectation, the circumstances and the way he speaks makes it so unreasonable.

In the UK, the test was also universally accepted. For example, in one leading privacy case, Campbell v MGN Ltd, Lord Hope commented that: â€˜â€¦ a duty of confidence will arise whenever the party subjects to the duty is in a situation where he knows or ought to know that the other person can reasonably expect his privacy to be protectedâ€™. Other judges also gave similar remarks.

One case that adopted the test is Associated Newspapers Ltd v His Royal Highness The Prince of Wales. In that case, the alleged privacy is a journal by Prince Charles. The journal recounts his visit to HK during the formal handover to China. Prince Charles has shown his journal to some of his friends, about 60 in number. Some of those who have seen the journal were journalists from the news industry. The journal was then leaked and published by the newspaper. As the journal contained words unfriendly to Chinese government and then president, it caused some diplomatic issues.

The case went to court as a case of breach of personal confidence. A successful claim of breach of confidence should establish 3 points, among which one is that â€˜the information itself â€¦ must â€œhave the necessary quality of confidence about itâ€â€™. Thus, the issue here is whether the journal is still confidential. It was held that despite the disclosure to some selected friends, it is still a confidential information.

According to the court, the number of persons who have been shown the journal is irrelevant. Instead the key should be the personâ€™s â€˜reasonable expectationâ€™. As long as Prince Charles reasonably expected that to be confidence, it then should be. The court went through the surrounding circumstances and the fact that Prince Charles never give consent to disclose those journals, and then concluded that those journals are still â€˜privateâ€™.

The latter case is of extreme value to our question at hand. As noted above, one issue faced in the determination of privacy in Social Medias was whether usersâ€™ action of posting, sharing, discussing publicly (for example, commenting or even starting a dialogue under othersâ€™ posts) might be still considered as privacy. In doing those acts they clearly knew that those posts or even conversations are visible at least to their â€˜friendsâ€™, yet still decided to do them. Can those information visible to still be â€˜privateâ€™? In other words, on Facebook (and other social medias as well), where is the fine line between socialization and publication?

The answer given by Associated Newspapers is that, in considering this issue, the court should assess the personâ€™s actions in full, and figure out whether they act with a â€˜reasonable expectationâ€™ of privacy.

However, the case does not give a simple and clear answer for social media scenarios. Firstly, is an expectation of privacy against, and only against, friendâ€™s parents, a â€˜reasonableâ€™ one? That is not easy to sort out. Indeed, it might probably be true that many, if not most, teenagers would argue strongly for a privacy against parents. Therefore, merely a majority view of the youth generation might not be enough to qualify its reasonableness.

Secondly, although not an express requirement under either UK law or US law, it is implied from the nature of privacy law that a violation of oneâ€™ privacy happens only if what is or ought to be private is now made no longer confidential. In other words, the only breach is to make it public, either by intrusions of public officials or by publications. Does the permission to parentsâ€™ access amount to a â€˜publicationâ€™?

One reasonable answer, arisen from the reasoning in Associated Newspapers, might be that, since disclosure to about 60 people does not affect its confidentiality, here giving access to privacy to 2 more (i.e. parents) would also not make it public. As a result, the privacy is not violated. However, the parents are under a duty to confidence.

V LESSONS AND PRIVACY IN THE DIGITAL ERA

The UK (and probably Australian) jurisdictional precedent did offer something for our analysis of the issue of privacy in the digital era. The equitable doctrine of confidential information is based on the idea of trust. It suggested that whether something is a privacy is based on the personâ€™s state of mind. If it passes the objective test (â€˜reasonableâ€™), then the others are imposed a duty not to make it public.

The idea of trust is eminent for the understanding of privacy in the digital era. This eminence is manifested in the fact that people are now always putting data on the server, other than in his/her own hard drives. The reason behind the trend is simply the idea of trust: I trust you, so I put my data in your server. I have confidence that you would not use my data for illegal purpose or for pursuit of unauthorised profits. Equity protects this kind of trust, by imposing duties on the data holder. It might be appropriate to characterise the relationship as a relationship of trust.

This can be contrasted with the US position. The characterisation of privacy as â€˜right to be let aloneâ€™ seems to be a little bit improper for the digital era. If the right to privacy is a â€˜right to be let aloneâ€™, then it would be weird that they are not â€˜letting aloneâ€™ themselves by those actions on the internet while persistently trying to claim this right.

Besides, in the US there was the famous, if not notorious, 3rd party doctrine, namely that â€˜an individual has no reasonable expectation of privacy in information voluntarily disclosed to 3rd partiesâ€™. This doctrine was developed and used in the US for obtaining evidences from 3rd parties. For example, in United States v Miller, it was held that police can apply for a court order or a subpoena to obtain a suspectâ€™s financial records from the bank. The reasoning by the court is that these information are not privacy, since they are â€˜voluntarily conveyed to the banks and exposed to their employees in the ordinary course of businessâ€™. Theoretically there is a distinction between metadata (e.g. the list of phone calls youâ€™ve made) and the data (e.g. the content of the conversation you made during the phone call, i.e. your privacy), and 3rd party doctrine can be justified on the ground that it applies to metadata only. However, in reality the application is obscure. The development in modern technology has made metadata more likely to be a privacy concern as well. For example, mobile service providers may provide information as to your geographic locations for the past period. Fortunately, the doctrine has been partly overruled in the recent US Supreme Court case Carpenter v United States, where the majority of the court said:
[S]eismic shifts in digital technology that made possible the tracking of not only Carpenterâ€™s location but also everyone elseâ€™s, not for a short period but for years and years â€¦ There is a world of difference between the limited types of personal information addressed in Smith and Miller and the exhaustive chronicle of location information casually collected by wireless carriers today â€¦

The idea of trust is suitable also in the sense that it fits well into the data protection schemes. In the digital age, data protection has been regarded as a crucial part of privacy that deserves preservation. The Facebook scandal of Cambridge Analytica reveals that.

This has resulted in the worldwide trend of data protection legislation, among which one prominent landmark piece of legislation is the General Data Protection Regulation (â€˜GDPRâ€™), which has recently come into force in EU. These data protections laws or regulations are a response to the increasing use of social media and the use of cloud.

One consequence of the large amount of legislation is that it enables the court to rule without giving any comment on the nature of privacy. One example is the US case Ajemian v Yahoo!. In that case, the plaintiff died and left a Yahoo! Email account but no will, no instructions for his account, and no record of his password. Pâ€™s siblings have been appointed by the court as personal reps of Plaintiffâ€™s estate. They asked Yahoo! For access to that account for 2 purposes: (1) to inform Pâ€™s friends for the passing and memorial service via the email; and perhaps more importantly (2) to identify the Pâ€™s assets.

Their argument had 2 basis: the first basis is the US federation legislation Stored Communications Act (â€˜SCAâ€™), which prohibits electronic-communication companies from disclosing a personâ€™s communications to third parties without his or her â€˜lawful consentâ€™; since no express consent was provided here, the request should be dismissed. What the supreme court of Massachusetts did was a strict statutory interpretation. Moreover, legislative history indicated that the enacting Congress intended for the SCA to apply to â€˜unauthorized interception of electronic communicationsâ€™ rather than estate management. This case was similar to the German case, in that both involves a deceasedâ€™s online account. Yet they are different in that this case was about an Emailbox, the court did not need to take in account other usersâ€™ privacy issues. Indeed, an Emailbox is similar to those inheritable properties, such as letters or diaries. Facebook chats are different. A more appropriate analogy to Facebook chats is oral conversations, where 2 partiesâ€™ privacy is involved.

But this case may still teach us something. What this case teaches us is that the general aim for those consent-requiring provisions were for the purpose of data security. When it comes to other issues, such as property management or financial or legal purposes, the court may not interpret the relevant provisions so strictly.

It follows that, those consent standards in other similar regulations, such as in the GDPR, should be interpreted in the same way. The focus should be put on limiting the government and the data holder, rather than on these relatives. Faced with the urgent issue of misuse by the data holders and the potential threat of collusion with the state government, the privacy concern arisen from parentsâ€™ access seems to be trivial. As German Scholar Buermeyer commented on the abovenoted German case: â€˜While the parents of the deceased had to spend years litigating to read their daughterâ€™s messages, the National Security Agency and the Federal Intelligence Service would only need a few mouse clicksâ€™.

To choose to use the method is clearly a manifestation of oneâ€™s expectation that the content should be privacy and there seems nothing â€˜unreasonableâ€™ to it.
